My Third Semester in HKU

Posted on 2019-09-30 | Post modified: 2020-04-13 | In 随笔 |


The third semester is a short summer semester, which lasts for two months only. Since I want to get my master degree by the end of this year, I need to take two courses and a final project.

(Updated on 2020-04-13)

Image Processing and Computer Vision

Dr. Dirk Schnieders is an excellent lecturer. He can explain any complicated things in a understandable way. This course is interesting and of great challenge. The first two courses are simple because they are all about human visual system. Sometimes it makes me wonder, is it necessary for a CS student to learn biological theory in such a comprehensive way? After the two “biology” chapter, it starts to get difficult. Most of the students find it difficult to deal with projection calculation. Luckily, I have been introduced to projection before, thanks to my undergraduate study, so it doesn’t bother me too much. As for image processing, there are a lot of techniques for object detection and image enhancement. Dr. Dirk pays a large money for an online coding platform, named Ed. It’s a great platform automatically assigns container (like docker) or virtual environment for user to run their codes. Dr. Dirk loves it so much that he says if anyone knows how to write these platform, he would love to quit his teaching job, go to found a company and make huge money. We have more than 10 image processing tasks in Ed written in Java. It takes me a lot of time to solve these tasks but I really enjoy it. I have to say that Java is not a good tool for math calculation, since it raises floating error from time to time (answer differs between fractional part, e.g. 100.3333 vs 100.33334).

Securities Transaction Banking

This course almost kill me. I thought it was about information security in banking, but I was totally wrong. Securities has nothing to do with security, and transactiong banking is not the common banking (saving money in the bank) as I thought it would be. As for a CS student with no financial background, I was confused in the very first lecture, and the next 9 lectures (ps, we have 10 lectures, 30 learning hours in total).

Part 1, Types of securities, financial markets, custody functions and regulatory requirements. Before I take this course, I don’t even know what bonds, stocks, or funds are, not to mention options and futures. Part 1 gives me a concept of what they are, and how they function.

Part 2, Transaction Banking. These part contains such a huge volume of knowledge I cannot conclude in a word or two. Three banking landscapes: privated owned banks (like HSBC), communal and federal banks (government-owned), and cooperative-owned banks (like agricultural banks). In another perspective, it can also be distincted based on the participation in the banking value chain: distribution banks (customer consultation, advice), production banks (e.g. insurance, fund houses, savings banks, investment banks), and transaction banks (focused on processing, standardized execution). Banks are controlled and monitored by a vast array of laws and regulations, e.g., Basel II, Sarbanes-Oxely Act (SOx).

Part 3, Internal Organisation of a Securities Transaction Bank. This part goes very quickly. It covers departmental structure of securities services: research, advice, order routing, clearing/settlement, custody service, print output, regulatory reporting, controlling audit.

Part 4, Information Technology in Transaction Banks. Here finally comes to the part that I am comfortable with. Since this is not a programming course, it only gives you a high level concept of how IT works in STB (securities transaction banking). It quickly goes through the pros and cons in the use of cloud services in this field, IT processes, data centre and information security.

This was the first time I was introduced to the term regulatory, or regulation, along with other works like KYC (know your customer). During the period, I happened to go to an interview of a company in the field of regulatory and compliance.
